Benjamin Franklin

By: Lola Ducroux

About Franklin

Benjamin Franklin (1706–1790) was one of the most influential figures in American history. A true Renaissance man, he was a polymath whose accomplishments spanned multiple fields, including science, philosophy, diplomacy, literature, and politics. Franklin was best known for his groundbreaking scientific work, especially his experiments with electricity, which earned him international fame. Not to mention his immense role in founding the United States of America. Franklin was a signer of the Declaration of Independence and contributed to the drafting of the U.S. Constitution. His wisdom and pragmatic approach helped steer the fledgling nation through its most formative years.In his later years, Franklin’s influence grew as an advocate for the values of liberty, democracy, and civic responsibility. His life story embodies the spirit of the American Enlightenment and helped lay the foundation for the modern United States.

Early Life

Benjamin Franklin was born on January 17, 1706, in Boston, Massachusetts, to Josiah Franklin and Abiah Folger. He was the 15th of 17 children, making his early life full of family. His father was a modest soap and candle maker, and his mother came from a prominent New England family. Franklin’s family was not wealthy, and they lived in a middle-class household, but his early environment would shape much of his future achievements.

Franklin received only two years of formal schooling, as his family could not afford to send him to school full time. He attended Boston Latin School briefly, but his formal education ended at age 10. However his academic drive read extensively, teaching himself through books, and developed a love for reading and writing.

At the age of 12, Franklin was apprenticed to his brother James Franklin, who was a printer. James ran a newspaper, the New England Courant, one of the first in the colonies. Benjamin worked as an apprentice, helping set type and working on the printing press. It was during this time that Franklin began to write anonymously for the paper, contributing essays and critiques. His wit, writing style, and intelligence impressed many readers, and it was in this period that Franklin's love of writing and public discourse truly began to take shape. However, after some time, Franklin’s relationship with his brother became strained. James Franklin was harsh with him, and Benjamin felt stifled by his brother’s authoritarian style. After three years of working under James, Benjamin ran away at age 17 to seek greater opportunities in Philadelphia, a move that would change the course of his life.

Life in Philadelphia

Franklin arrived in Philadelphia in 1723 with only a few dollars in his pocket. His first days in the city were difficult, but he found work as a printer. After a few years, he gained experience and began to rise in prominence, eventually opening his own printing business. Philadelphia became his base of operations for the rest of his life.

In 1729, he bought a struggling newspaper, the Pennsylvania Gazette, which under his direction became one of the most successful in the colonies. Franklin’s writing, particularly his satirical essays and "Poor Richard's Almanack", published annually from 1732 to 1758, helped solidify his reputation as a public intellectual and a master of both humor and practical advice.

One of the most unforgettable moments in Franklin's Autobiography occurs when he arrives in Philadelphia on a Sunday morning, exhausted and famished. He stops at a bakery and asks for three pennies' worth of bread, only to receive "three large, puffy rolls." Carrying one under each arm and nibbling on the third, he walks up Market Street past the Read family’s door, where he catches the eye of Deborah, his future wife. She saw him and "thought, as I certainly did, that I made a most awkward and ridiculous appearance."

Franklin's growing success as a printer allowed him to focus more on civic projects. He helped establish the Library Company of Philadelphia in 1731, the first public library in the colonies, and founded the American Philosophical Society in 1743.He became deeply interested in science, particularly in electricity, which would lead to some of his most groundbreaking work.

Scientific Achievments

During the 1730s and 1740s, Franklin’s interests expanded into science and philosophy. He conducted a variety of scientific experiments, particularly in the field of electricity, which would become his most well-known area of expertise.

His famous kite experiment took place in Philadelphia, in June 1752. Franklin asked his son William to fly a silf kite, to which he had attached a wire on top and a key at the end of a string. Eventually, the kite (and the key) amassed an electric charge from a storm cloud. Franklin was able to collect some of the charge and compared it to the electricity he had created in other experiments. However he did not publicly publish his experiment until October, at which point Franklin did not clarify that he had conducted the experiment himself, an issue that has caused some scholars to doubt his story.

Franklin also invented the Franklin stove, a more efficient heating system, and the lightning rod, after his kite experiment. Franklin’s electrical experiments propelled him into a global network of scholars, both men and women, who were pioneering work in fields we now recognize as physics, chemistry, biology, botany, and paleontology.

The image above, "Join, or Die" is one of the most famous political cartoons created by Benjamin Franklin in 1754, during the early stages of the French and Indian War. The image depicts a segmented snake, with each segment labeled to represent one of the American colonies or regions. The message was clear: the colonies needed to unite or face destruction, symbolized by the snake being cut into pieces. Franklin originally used the cartoon in his publication Pennsylvania Gazette to advocate for colonial unity under the Albany Plan of Union, which proposed a collective government to address common challenges. The image became a symbol of colonial unity during the American Revolution, used to encourage the colonies to band together in the fight against Britain.

Political Work

Although Benjamin Franklin's electrical experiments brought him great success, he never considered science to be as important as public service.

He became a member of the Philadelphia City Council in 1748, justice of the peace in 1749, and in 1751 a city alderman and a member of the Pennsylvania Assembly.

In 1753, Franklin was appointed deputy postmaster general, a royal office overseeing the mail system across all the northern colonies. This position marked a turning point in his thinking, as he began to adopt a broader, intercolonial perspective on issues affecting the colonies as a whole.

Benjamin Franklin established the Albany Plan of Union in 1754 at the Albany Congress, aimed at creating a unified government for the American colonies. It sought to establish a Grand Council made up of representatives from each colony, with the power to levy taxes, raise armies, and manage colonial defense. The plan was designed to strengthen the colonies’ collective security against the French and Native American threats during the French and Indian War. However, it was rejected by both the colonial assemblies and the British government, as the colonies were unwilling to surrender any authority, and Britain was reluctant to grant too much power to the colonies. Despite its failure, the Albany Plan was an early step toward ideas of colonial unity that would later influence the creation of the U.S. Constitution.

More significantly, as the diplomatic representative of the newly-formed American republic in France during the Revolution, Franklin secured diplomatic recognition and essential financial and military support from Louis XVI's government. He was also a key member of the commission that negotiated the treaty in which Great Britain acknowledged the independence of its former 13 colonies. Given that no one else could have achieved all that Franklin did in France during this critical period, he can justifiably be seen as America's greatest diplomat.

Legacy

Franklin’s legacy is profound and multifaceted. He is remembered as one of the most brilliant and versatile men in American history. His contributions to science, particularly in electricity, and his role in securing American independence and crafting its founding documents ensure his place as a key architect of the United States. Franklin's sayings, his inventions, and his writings continue to influence both American culture and the broader world today.
